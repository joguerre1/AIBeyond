The Solution: The FBI Framework

Simon Sinek proposes a simple, three-step method to initiate a conversation non-confrontationally. The goal is to state your perspective in a way that invites dialogue, not defense.

F

Feelings

Start with your feeling. It's your truth and cannot be disputed.

→
B

Behavior

Describe the specific, observable behavior that caused the feeling.

→
I

Impact

Explain the tangible impact that behavior has on you or your work.

Step 1 (Feelings): Disarm with "I"

Starting with "You..." puts people on the defensive. Starting with "I feel..." is an invitation to understand your perspective. The perceived threat level drops dramatically.

Step 2 (Behavior): Be Specific

Avoid generalizations and accusations. Stick to facts about what you observed. This removes judgment and focuses the conversation on a concrete event.

Vague & Accusatory Specific & Factual
"You're always undermining me." "Yesterday in the team meeting..."
"You ignored my email." "When I saw my email was unread..."
"You're being disrespectful." "When you spoke over me..."

Step 3 (Impact): Connect the Dots

Explain the consequences. This helps the other person understand why their behavior was problematic, moving it from a minor action to something with real-world effects.

FBI in Action: A Case Study

Confrontational Approach

"Why did you throw my project under the bus in front of the client? You're always trying to make me look bad."

The FBI Approach

[F] "I felt really blindsided and embarrassed... [B] ...when you brought up the budget issue for the first time during the client call... [I] ...because it made it seem like our team isn't aligned, and I'm worried it undermined the client's confidence in us."

The Positive Outcomes

Teams that handle conflict constructively see measurable improvements in culture and performance.
